Q1 · Effect of the effective horizon 8 pts

An inventory MDP: stock s ∈ {0,…,10}. Sell gives +1 and s→s−1 (for s>0). Buy gives 0 and s→s+1, except the transition 9→10 gives +100, and s=10 is terminal. Start at s=3.

Intuition — the whole problem in one line There are only two ways to earn reward: sell your way down (+1 each, at most +3 from s=3 before you hit 0), or buy your way up to the +100 jackpot at state 10 (which needs 7 buys minimum). Everything below is bookkeeping on that trade-off.

(a) Can some horizon H make the optimal policy take both buy and sell?

Yes. This is the counter-intuitive part. To reach the jackpot you need net +7 in position. But if you have extra horizon to spare, you can first sell (bank a +1) and then buy back — each such detour costs 2 steps and nets an extra +1, and it's worth it as long as you still have enough steps left to reach state 10.

Formally, sell k times (k≤3, staying ≥0), then buy up to 10. That path uses   k + (7+k) = 7+2k steps and earns 100 + k. So the optimum is to take the largest k with 7+2k ≤ H and k≤3.

for H = 9:   sell once (3→2, +1), then buy 8× (2→10, +100)  =  +101 > 100

So H=9 (and 11, 13) yields an optimal policy that sells and buys. For any H≥9 with slack, the optimum mixes both actions.

(b) For what H does the optimum reach a fully stocked inventory (s=10)?

H ≥ 7. Reaching 10 requires 7 buys minimum, so it's impossible for H<7. For any H≥7, the buy-to-10 path earns ≥100, which dominates the best sell-only payoff (+3). So the optimal policy fully stocks for exactly the range H ≥ 7.

(c) Infinite horizon, γ = 0. Optimal action at s=3? at s=9?

γ=0 means only the immediate reward counts — the agent is fully myopic.

  • s=3: sell → +1 now, buy → 0 now ⟹ sell.
  • s=9: buy → +100 now (the 9→10 transition), sell → +1 now ⟹ buy.

(d) Can a fixed γ ∈ [0,1) make the optimum starting from s=3 never fully stock?

Yes. The jackpot is collected only after 7 buys, so its discounted value from s=3 is ≈ γ6·100, while selling banks 1 + γ + γ² immediately. For small enough γ the near, certain +1 beats the far, heavily-discounted +100, so the greedy policy sells forever and never reaches 10. The extreme case γ=0 (part c) already shows it: the agent sells every step and never buys up. So such a γ exists.

Concept refresher — the "effective horizon" Discounting by γ behaves like a soft horizon of roughly 11−γ steps. A distant reward R that is d steps away is worth only γdR today. Both the hard horizon H and the discount γ change how far the agent is willing to look, which is why the same reward + dynamics can produce completely different optimal policies.

Q2 · Reward hacking 5 pts

Autonomous-car merging. True goal: minimize mean commute. Proxy reward: maximize mean velocity of all cars. One AI car (red), many human cars (grey).

(a) Why is it optimal for the AI car not to merge?

Merging onto a busy highway forces the many human cars to brake and bunch up — a slowdown wave that lowers the velocity of lots of cars. Keeping the single AI car off the road lets the human traffic flow at high speed. Because the proxy averages velocity over all cars, sacrificing one car's contribution to keep many cars fast yields a higher mean velocity than merging. So under the proxy, "park and never merge" is optimal — even though the car accomplishes nothing.

(b) Alternate rewards that are still easy to optimize but don't produce the park-forever policy.

The failure is that the proxy ignores whether the AI car completes its own trip. Fixes that reintroduce that term while staying tractable: (1) reward the AI car for its own progress toward its destination, e.g. r = distance-to-goal reduced this step, so parking earns nothing; (2) optimize throughput — number of cars reaching their destinations per unit time — which requires the AI to actually travel; (3) penalize each car's total travel time (a proxy for commute) rather than instantaneous velocity, so an idle car accrues cost. Any of these keeps the "keep traffic flowing" signal but makes never-merging strictly suboptimal.

Q3 · Bellman residuals & performance bounds 30 pts

Concept refresher — the two Bellman operators For a value vector V ∈ ℝ|S|:
(BV)(s) = maxa [ r(s,a) + γ Σs' p(s'|s,a) V(s') ]   (optimality operator, fixed point V∗)
(BπV)(s) = r(s,π(s)) + γ Σs' p(s'|s,π(s)) V(s')   (policy operator, fixed point Vπ)
Both are γ-contractions in the sup-norm ‖v‖ = maxs|v(s)|. Key distinction: V is any vector; Vπ is a vector actually achievable by a policy.

(a) Show ‖BπV − BπV′‖ ≤ γ‖V − V′‖.

Proof∎ The reward terms cancel, leaving for every s:
(BπV)(s) − (BπV′)(s) = γ Σs' p(s'|s,π(s)) [V(s') − V′(s')]
Take absolute value and push it inside the sum (triangle inequality), then bound each difference by the max:
|·| ≤ γ Σs' p(s'|s,π(s)) ‖V−V′‖ = γ‖V−V′‖
because the transition probabilities sum to 1. This holds for every s, so taking the max over s on the left gives ‖BπV − BπV′‖ ≤ γ‖V−V′‖.

(b) Prove the fixed point of Bπ is unique.

Proof (by contradiction)∎ Suppose V1 and V2 are both fixed points with V1 ≠ V2, so ‖V1−V2‖ > 0. Since each equals its own backup, part (a) gives
‖V1−V2‖ = ‖BπV1 − BπV2‖ ≤ γ‖V1−V2‖.
Dividing by the positive quantity ‖V1−V2‖ yields 1 ≤ γ, contradicting γ < 1. Hence V1 = V2.

(c) If V ≤ V′ elementwise, show BπV ≤ BπV′ (monotonicity).

Proof∎
(BπV′)(s) − (BπV)(s) = γ Σs' p(s'|s,π(s)) [V′(s') − V(s')] ≥ 0,
since every bracket is ≥ 0 and every probability is ≥ 0. So BπV(s) ≤ BπV′(s) for all s. (The same argument works for the optimality operator B, because the max of order-preserving maps is order-preserving.)

(d) For what V is the Bellman error ‖BV − V‖ = 0? Why?

Exactly V = V∗, the optimal value function. ‖BV−V‖ = 0 ⟺ BV = V ⟺ V is a fixed point of B. Since B is a contraction it has a unique fixed point, and that fixed point is V∗ by definition. So only the optimal value function has zero Bellman error.

(e) Prove ‖V−Vπ‖ ≤ ‖V−BπV‖ / (1−γ)  and  ‖V−V∗‖ ≤ ‖V−BV‖ / (1−γ).

Proof∎ Insert the "zero term" −BπV + BπV and use the triangle inequality. Since Vπ = BπVπ:
‖V−Vπ‖ ≤ ‖V−BπV‖ + ‖BπV−Vπ‖ = ‖V−BπV‖ + ‖BπV−BπVπ‖
The last term is ≤ γ‖V−Vπ‖ by part (a). Collecting the ‖V−Vπ‖ terms:
(1−γ)‖V−Vπ‖ ≤ ‖V−BπV‖  ⟹  ‖V−Vπ‖ ≤ ‖V−BπV‖1−γ.
Repeating verbatim with B and its fixed point V∗ gives the second inequality.

(f) Let π be greedy w.r.t. V, and ε = ‖BV−V‖. Prove Vπ(s) ≥ V∗(s) − 2ε/(1−γ).

Key fact used everywhere below If π is greedy with respect to V, then π chooses exactly the action attaining the max in BV. Therefore BπV = BV. This one equality links the policy operator to the optimality operator.
Proof∎ Add and subtract V(s) and use the triangle inequality:
V∗(s) − Vπ(s) ≤ |V∗(s)−V(s)| + |V(s)−Vπ(s)| ≤ ‖V−V∗‖ + ‖V−Vπ‖
Bound each with part (e). The first: ‖V−V∗‖ ≤ ‖BV−V‖/(1−γ) = ε/(1−γ). The second, using BπV=BV: ‖V−Vπ‖ ≤ ‖V−BπV‖/(1−γ) = ε/(1−γ). Adding,
V∗(s) − Vπ(s) ≤ 2ε1−γ  ⟹  Vπ(s) ≥ V∗(s) − 2ε1−γ.

(g) A real-world use for a lower bound on Vπ(s).

Any safety- or cost-critical deployment where you must certify performance before going live: medical treatment policies, financial/portfolio allocation, autonomous driving, datacenter control. You run approximate value iteration, measure the Bellman error ε of the returned V, and the bound in (f) guarantees the deployed greedy policy is within 2ε/(1−γ) of optimal — without ever knowing V∗. That converts "the algorithm converged" into a hard worst-case guarantee.

(h) Another V′ with greedy π′ and the same ε. Does the bound imply Vπ(s) = Vπ′(s) anywhere?

No. The bound is only a lower bound. Both Vπ and Vπ′ satisfy the same inequality ≥ V∗ − 2ε/(1−γ), but sharing a lower bound says nothing about equality — two different value functions can both sit above the same floor. Equal Bellman-error magnitude does not pin down the policy or its value.

(i) If additionally V∗ ≤ V, prove the tighter Vπ(s) ≥ V∗(s) − ε/(1−γ).

Proof∎ Recall every policy satisfies Vπ ≤ V∗ (optimality of V∗), and here V∗ ≤ V, so Vπ ≤ V and thus V−Vπ ≥ 0. Split the gap:
V∗(s) − Vπ(s) = [V∗(s)−V(s)] + [V(s)−Vπ(s)]
The first bracket is ≤ 0 (since V ≥ V∗). The second equals |V(s)−Vπ(s)| ≤ ‖V−Vπ‖ ≤ ε/(1−γ) as in (f). Hence V∗(s) − Vπ(s) ≤ ε/(1−γ). The extra assumption kills one of the two ε/(1−γ) terms — halving the bound.

(j) & (k) Challenge parts (ungraded).

(j) If BV ≤ V then V∗ ≤ V∎ B is monotone (part c analog). Apply it to BV ≤ V: B²V ≤ BV ≤ V. By induction BnV ≤ V for all n. Because B is a contraction, limn→∞ BnV = V∗. Taking the limit of both sides preserves ≤, giving V∗ ≤ V. This is useful because BV ≤ V is checkable without knowing V∗.
(k) Tighter γε bounds∎ Apply one extra Bellman backup. Using BπV=BV, Vπ=BπVπ, V∗=BV∗:
V∗(s)−Vπ(s) = [V∗(s)−(BπV)(s)] + [(BπV)(s)−Vπ(s)]
The first bracket = (BV∗)(s)−(BV)(s) ≤ γ‖V∗−V‖ ≤ γε/(1−γ). The second = (BπV)(s)−(BπVπ)(s) ≤ γ‖V−Vπ‖ ≤ γε/(1−γ). Sum: Vπ(s) ≥ V∗(s) − 2γε/(1−γ). If V∗ ≤ V, the first bracket is ≤ 0 (monotonicity), leaving the single-term bound Vπ(s) ≥ V∗(s) − γε/(1−γ).
The big picture of Q3 A constant per-step reward r compounds to r/(1−γ) of return. So an ε-Bellman-error value function yields a greedy policy whose average per-step reward is off by at most ~2ε. Reduce the Bellman residual → automatically bound how good the extracted policy is. That is the theoretical license behind every approximate-VI / approximate-DP method.

Small reward 0.005 for LEFT at state 0, big reward 1.0 for RIGHT at state 5. Swimming right succeeds w.p. 0.6 stay / (0.4 − 0.09·c) forward / 0.09·c back, where current c ∈ {1,2,3}. A1_RiverSwim_MDP/vi_and_pi.py

(a) bellman_backup

def bellman_backup(state, action, R, T, gamma, V):
    backup_val = R[state, action] + gamma * np.sum(T[state, action] * V)
    return backup_val

A direct transcription of Q(s,a) = r(s,a) + γ Σs' T(s'|s,a)V(s'). T[state,action] is a length-6 probability vector; T[state,action] * V is the elementwise product and np.sum collapses it to the expected next-state value. Correct and clean.

(b) policy_evaluation, policy_improvement, policy_iteration

# policy_evaluation: iterate V ← B^π V until it stops moving
V_last = np.ones(num_states)                # seed ≠ value_function so the loop starts
while np.linalg.norm(V_last - value_function, np.inf) > tol:
    V_last = np.copy(value_function)         # deep copy — important!
    for i in range(num_states):
        value_function[i] = bellman_backup(i, policy[i], R, T, gamma, V_last)

# policy_improvement: greedy action per state (vectorized argmax over Q)
new_policy = np.argmax(R + gamma * np.sum(T * V_policy, axis=2), axis=1)

# policy_iteration: alternate evaluate / improve until the policy is stable
while True:
    V_policy   = policy_evaluation(policy, R, T, gamma, tol)
    new_policy = policy_improvement(policy, R, T, V_policy, gamma)
    if np.linalg.norm(new_policy - policy) > tol:
        policy = new_policy
    else:
        break
Concept refresher — policy iteration Two nested loops. Evaluation solves Vπ = BπVπ for the current π (a fixed-point iteration). Improvement makes π greedy w.r.t. that Vπ. The policy-improvement theorem guarantees each round is a monotone improvement, and with finitely many policies it converges to π∗ in a small number of rounds.

The vectorized policy_improvement is the elegant bit: T is (S,A,S), T * V_policy broadcasts V over the last axis, np.sum(..., axis=2) gives expected next-state values shaped (S,A), add R, and argmax(axis=1) picks the best action per state.

Minor issue — convergence test on integer policies np.linalg.norm(new_policy - policy) > tol compares two integer action vectors with a float tolerance. It happens to work (identical policies → norm 0 < tol; any change → norm ≥ 1 > tol), but it's semantically odd. Prefer if not np.array_equal(new_policy, policy): policy = new_policy; else: break — that's exactly "loop until the policy stops changing" and can't be tripped up by the tolerance.

(c) value_iteration

while True:
    last_v = np.copy(value_function)
    for i in range(num_states):
        value_function[i] = np.max(R[i] + gamma * np.sum(T[i] * last_v, axis=1))
    policy = np.argmax(R + gamma * np.sum(T * value_function, axis=2), axis=1)
    if np.linalg.norm(last_v - value_function, np.inf) < tol:
        break

VI applies the optimality operator directly: V(s) ← maxa Q(s,a). It never explicitly stores a policy during the loop — it just contracts V toward V∗ — then reads off the greedy policy once. Correct. (The inner per-state for could also be fully vectorized like the policy line, but 6 states makes it irrelevant.)

Q1 · Deep Q-Networks (written) 8 pts

(a) Three key differences between DQN and tabular Q-learning.

  1. Function approximation. DQN approximates Q(s,a;θ) with a deep neural net (generalizes across states, handles huge/continuous observation spaces); tabular Q-learning stores one number per (s,a).
  2. Experience replay. DQN stores transitions in a buffer and trains on random minibatches, decorrelating consecutive samples and reusing data; Q-learning updates online from the single most-recent transition.
  3. Target network. DQN computes bootstrap targets with a separate, periodically-synced network Q̂(·;θ−); Q-learning bootstraps off the same, constantly-moving Q. The frozen target stabilizes the regression.

(b) Which component contributes most to performance gains? Justify.

Experience replay is the single biggest contributor. The Nature-2015 DQN ablation removed each trick in turn: dropping replay caused by far the largest score collapse across games, larger than removing the target network. Two reasons: (1) it breaks the strong temporal correlation of on-policy trajectories, which otherwise makes SGD unstable and biased; (2) it reuses each transition many times, hugely improving sample efficiency. The target network matters too (it prevents the "chasing a moving target" divergence), but replay is what makes non-linear function approximation trainable at all.

(c) What if the target network is updated only every ~10¹⁵ steps for a simple game like Pong?

With such an astronomically large period C, θ− is effectively frozen at its random initialization for the entire run. Every target yj = rj + γ max Q̂(sj+1,·;θ−) is computed from a fixed, meaningless network, so improvements in Q never propagate into the targets it is regressing toward. Reward information cannot bootstrap backward through time; learning stalls and the agent never gets good at Pong. (The opposite failure — updating every 10–15 steps — is also bad: the target moves almost as fast as Q, reintroducing the oscillation/divergence the target network was meant to cure. The sweet spot is an intermediate C.)

Q2 · Policy gradient — coding & writeup 54 + 26 pts

Concept refresher — the policy gradient
∇θJ(θ) = 𝔼πθ[ ∇θ log πθ(a|s) · Qπ(s,a) ]
REINFORCE replaces Qπ with the sampled return Gt (unbiased, high variance). Subtracting a state-dependent baseline b(s) ≈ V(s) leaves the gradient unbiased but cuts variance; the result Ât = Gt − b(st) is an advantage estimate.

Concept refresher — why log_std, not std Standard deviation must be positive. Parameterizing log_std freely in ℝ and exponentiating guarantees σ = elog_std > 0 with no constraints on the optimizer. Keeping it a state-independent nn.Parameter (rather than a network output) is the common, stable choice for continuous control.
A confusing-but-correct line worth pausing on std = torch.square(self.std()) reads like a bug — it's squaring the standard deviation. But MultivariateNormal's covariance_matrix argument wants variances on the diagonal, and variance = σ². So the math is right; only the variable name is misleading. Rename it var for clarity. Also, .numpy() in act assumes CPU tensors — on a GPU it would throw; use .detach().cpu().numpy() for portability.

The get_returns reverse pass is precisely the answer to writeup (a) below. update_policy negates the objective because PyTorch minimizes — minimizing −J maximizes J. Dividing by the batch size makes the step size insensitive to how many transitions landed in the batch.

code/ppo.py · update_policy

dist = self.policy.action_distribution(observations)
log_probs = dist.log_prob(actions)
ratio = torch.exp(log_probs - old_logprobs)                        # z = π_θ / π_old
clipped = torch.clip(ratio, 1 - self.eps_clip, 1 + self.eps_clip)
loss = -torch.sum(torch.minimum(ratio * advantages,
                                clipped * advantages)) / T          # clipped surrogate
loss.backward(); self.optimizer.step()
Concept refresher — PPO in one idea REINFORCE is on-policy: collect a batch, take one step, throw the batch away. PPO squeezes several updates out of each batch by importance-weighting with the ratio z = πθ/πold, and clipping that ratio to [1−ε, 1+ε] so no single update drifts too far off-policy. The min makes the surrogate a pessimistic (lower) bound on the true improvement.

Note in ppo.py the rollout caches old_logprobs at collection time, and the training loop runs update_freq inner updates per batch (the "reuse the batch" behavior). Both are the reason PPO needs the cached log-probs — see writeup (c).

Writeup (a) Compute all {Gt} in O(T) instead of O(T²).

Accumulate backward: GT = rT, and Gt = rt + γGt+1. One right-to-left pass reuses the already-computed suffix sum, so each of the T returns costs O(1) — total O(T). That is exactly the reverse loop in get_returns.

Writeup (b) When is the gradient of the clipped PPO loss zero, and why?

Let z be the ratio and Â the advantage. Working through the min and clip:

∇ = 0  ⟺  (Â > 0 and z ≥ 1+ε)  or  (Â < 0 and z ≤ 1−ε)

In those regions the clipped branch (1±ε)·Â — a constant in θ — is the one selected by the min, so its derivative vanishes. Why: when the advantage is positive and the policy has already increased this action's probability past 1+ε, PPO stops rewarding further increases — it refuses to push an already-large, already-beneficial update even larger. Symmetrically for Â<0 once probability has dropped below 1−ε. But note the asymmetry: if Â>0 and z<1−ε (an overshoot in the wrong direction), the unclipped term is the min and the gradient is nonzero — PPO still pulls back. Clipping caps beneficial-direction steps but never stops corrective ones.

Writeup (c) Why does REINFORCE not cache the sampling log-prob, but PPO must?

REINFORCE takes exactly one gradient step per batch using the current policy. The log π(a|s) it needs is recomputed live from the current network (and must stay differentiable), so there is nothing to cache. PPO instead performs multiple updates per batch and needs the ratio z = πθ/πold, where πold is the fixed policy that generated the data. Its log-probs are constants, so they're recorded at rollout time and detached. If they hadn't been collected: before each round of updates you'd have to snapshot the old policy's parameters (a frozen copy or saved state_dict) and recompute old_logprobs = old_policy.log_prob(actions).detach() from that copy — extra bookkeeping and compute that caching avoids.

Writeup (d) Run all three methods across seeds/envs and comment.

The repo already contains completed runs under code/results/ (CartPole, seeds 1–3, for baseline / no-baseline / ppo). Expected story, consistent with the sanity checks:

  • No baseline: learns but with high variance — noisy, oscillating return curves, slower to reach the ceiling.
  • Baseline: markedly lower variance and faster, more stable climb (the baseline is doing its variance-reduction job).
  • PPO: most sample-efficient per environment step because it reuses each batch for several updates; stable thanks to clipping.
  • All three should hit the targets: 200 on CartPole, 1000 on Pendulum, ≥200 on Cheetah, even if they don't hold the peak.

Q3 · Distributions induced by a policy 13 pts

(a) Probability ρπ(τ) of a trajectory τ = (s0,a0,s1,…).

ρπ(τ) = ∏t=0∞ π(at|st) · P(st+1|st,at)

The starting state s0 is fixed, so it contributes a factor of 1. Each step multiplies the probability of the chosen action (from the policy) by the probability of the resulting transition (from the dynamics).

(b) pπ(st = s): probability of being in s at time t.

Marginalize the trajectory distribution over everything except st:

pπ(st=s) = Σs0:t−1, a0:t−1 𝟙[st=s] ∏k=0t−1 π(ak|sk) P(sk+1|sk,ak)

(Equivalently the recursion pπ(s' at t+1) = Σs,a pπ(s at t) π(a|s) P(s'|s,a), starting from pπ(s0)=𝟙[s=s0].)

(c) Prove 𝔼τ~ρπ[Σt γt f(st,at)] = 11−γ 𝔼s~dπ[ 𝔼a~π(s)[f(s,a)] ].

Proof∎ Swap the (absolutely convergent) sum and expectation, then note the time-t term depends on τ only through the marginal (st,at):
LHS = Σt γt 𝔼[f(st,at)] = Σt γt Σs pπ(st=s) Σa π(a|s) f(s,a)
Exchange the t and s sums and pull the inner action-expectation out:
= Σs [ Σt γt pπ(st=s) ] 𝔼a~π(s)[f(s,a)]
The bracket is exactly dπ(s)/(1−γ) by the definition dπ(s) = (1−γ)Σtγtpπ(st=s). Substituting gives 11−γ Σs dπ(s) 𝔼a~π(s)[f] = RHS. (Sanity: f≡1 gives Σγt=1/(1−γ) on both sides.)

(d) Prove the performance-difference lemma: Vπ(s0) − Vπ′(s0) = 11−γ 𝔼s~dπ[ 𝔼a~π(s)[Aπ′(s,a)] ].

Proof∎ Take a trajectory generated by π from s0 and expand the advantage of the other policy π′ using Aπ′(s,a) = r(s,a) + γ𝔼[Vπ′(s')] − Vπ′(s):
𝔼τ~ρπ[ Σt γt Aπ′(st,at) ] = 𝔼[ Σt γt rt ] + 𝔼[ Σt ( γt+1Vπ′(st+1) − γtVπ′(st) ) ]
The first term is Vπ(s0). The second is a telescoping sum: everything cancels except the very first t=0 term, leaving −Vπ′(s0). So the left side equals Vπ(s0) − Vπ′(s0). Now apply part (c) with f = Aπ′ to rewrite the left side as 11−γ 𝔼s~dπ𝔼a~π[Aπ′]. Equate.
Why this matters This identity is the theoretical backbone of TRPO and PPO: it expresses the improvement of a new policy π (=πθ after an update) over the old π′ purely in terms of the old policy's advantage, averaged under the new policy's state distribution. Controlling how far π moves from π′ (the clip / KL constraint) controls how trustworthy this estimate is — which is exactly what the clipped objective in Q2 enforces.

Q4 · Ethics of policy gradients (RL on humans) 5 pts

(a) Two design choices that respect the Belmont principles.

Choice 1 — informed opt-in with the ability to withdraw (Respect for persons). Students should be told they may be assigned an experimental AI tutor of varying quality, consent explicitly, and be able to leave the study for full human help at any time. Without this, participants are enrolled in an experiment affecting their grades without agency over their own education — a direct violation of respect for persons/autonomy.

Choice 2 — a safety net that equalizes downside risk (Beneficence / Justice). Guarantee every student a floor of quality help — e.g. any student who received the (possibly weaker) early-stage chatbot can revise with a human CA before grading, or grades are curved to remove condition effects. Otherwise the harm of poor early-model advice falls unequally on whoever happened to be assigned more chatbot hours early in the term, violating justice (unequal distribution of risk) and beneficence (failing to minimize harm).

(b) What Stanford process gives clearance?

Because it experiments on human subjects, it requires IRB approval before any data collection. At Stanford that means preparing a research protocol and submitting it to the Stanford IRB / Research Compliance Office through the eProtocol system for review and approval, and not beginning the study until approval is granted.

Q1 · Reward engineering 6 + 7 pts

(a) Why is reward engineering hard? Risks? Give a deceptively-adequate reward.

It's hard because human intent is rich, contextual, and multi-objective, but a reward must be a single scalar defined over states/actions the designer can measure — so the map from "what we want" to "what we can score" is lossy. The risk is specification gaming / reward hacking: the agent maximizes the literal reward through behavior the designer never intended, sometimes catastrophically. Example: a vacuum robot rewarded for "amount of dust collected" learns to dump collected dust back out and re-vacuum it, maximizing throughput while never cleaning the room. (Classic real instance: the CoastRunners boat rewarded for score loops forever collecting power-ups instead of finishing the race.)

(b) Hopper: describe the goal and each reward term.

Goal: make a one-legged "hopper" hop forward as fast as possible without falling over. The reward is a sum of three terms: (1) a healthy/alive bonus — a positive constant for each timestep the hopper stays in its valid upright configuration, encouraging it not to fall; (2) a forward-progress reward — proportional to forward (+x) velocity, the actual objective; (3) a control cost — a negative penalty on the squared action/torque, encouraging energy-efficient, smooth movement rather than flailing. (Reasonable critique: the alive bonus can dominate early, teaching the agent to stand still and survive rather than hop; one could down-weight it or ramp the forward term.)

(c) What are "healthy" states? One advantage and one disadvantage of early termination.

"Healthy" is a box of state constraints — torso height within a range, joint angles/state values bounded and finite — meaning the hopper is upright and hasn't collapsed. Leaving that set ends the episode. Advantage: it stops wasting samples simulating a fallen, unrecoverable robot, so learning is faster and the alive bonus + termination together give a clear "don't fall" signal. Disadvantage: it biases the learned task — the agent never experiences near-fall recovery states (it's terminated first), so it can learn overly conservative behavior, and the survival incentive interacts with the alive bonus to sometimes reward timid hopping. It also makes returns across episodes of different lengths harder to compare.

(d) (coding) Train PPO on Hopper with/without early termination, 3 seeds.

Handled by the provided ppo_hopper.py (uses Stable-Baselines3 PPO). The repo's results/ contains both conditions for seed 1 with saved scores, plots, and rollout videos. The point of the exercise is to observe (writeup e–g) that early termination trains faster in wall-clock/epochs, and to inspect whether the resulting gait is what you'd expect.

Q2 · Learning from preferences (RLHF) 5 + 25 pts

Concept refresher — Bradley-Terry preference model Given two trajectory segments, model the probability that σ1 is preferred as a softmax over their summed predicted rewards:
P̂(σ1 ≻ σ2) = exp Σt r̂(o1t,a1t)exp Σ r̂1 + exp Σ r̂2 = σ(R1 − R2)
where Ri = Σt r̂(oit,ait) and σ is the sigmoid. Fit r̂ by cross-entropy against human labels — it's logistic regression on reward differences.

Written (a) With r̂(o,a) = ϕw(o,a), compute ∇w loss.

Derivation∎ For one labeled pair, loss = −[ μ(1) log σ(R1−R2) + μ(2) log σ(R2−R1) ]. Use ddx log σ(x) = 1 − σ(x) and write p = P̂(σ1≻σ2) = σ(R1−R2). Since ∇w(R2−R1) = −∇w(R1−R2):
∇w loss = −[ μ(1)(1−p) − μ(2)p ] ∇w(R1−R2)
With μ(1)+μ(2)=1, the bracket simplifies: μ(1)(1−p) − μ(2)p = μ(1) − p. Therefore
∇w loss = ( p − μ(1) ) Σt [ ∇wϕw(o1t,a1t) − ∇wϕw(o2t,a2t) ]
the classic logistic form (prediction − target) × (feature difference), summed over the dataset.

(f) Is the learned reward function identifiable?

No. The Bradley-Terry likelihood depends only on reward differences of trajectory sums, so any transformation that preserves those differences leaves the loss unchanged: adding a constant to r̂ (it cancels in R1−R2 over equal-length segments) and, more generally, positive rescaling (absorbed by the sigmoid's temperature) all give identical preferences. An entire family of reward functions is optimal, so only relative/ordinal reward is recovered, not an absolute scale — the classic RLHF identifiability caveat.

Q3 · Direct preference optimization (DPO) 25 pts

Concept refresher — DPO in one equation DPO skips the reward model entirely and optimizes the policy directly on preferences:
LDPO = −𝔼(x,yw,yl)[ log σ( β log πθ(yw|x)πref(yw|x) − β log πθ(yl|x)πref(yl|x) ) ]
It raises the (reference-relative) log-prob of the preferred sequence yw and lowers the rejected yl, with β controlling how far πθ may drift from πref. Here "sequences" are T-step action plans; the policy re-plans each step and executes only the first action (receding-horizon control).

(a) ActionSequenceModel — predict a distribution over the next T actions.

A3_RLHF_DPO/run_dpo.py · ActionSequenceModel

output_dim = 2 * action_dim * segment_len   # mean AND log-std for every (t, action-dim)
self.net = nn.Sequential(nn.Linear(obs_dim, hidden_dim), nn.ReLU(),
                         nn.Linear(hidden_dim, output_dim))

def forward(self, obs):
    first, second = torch.split(self.net(obs), self.segment_len*self.action_dim, dim=-1)
    mean    = torch.tanh(first.reshape(B, seg_len, act_dim))          # actions ∈ (-1,1)
    log_std = torch.clamp(second.reshape(B, seg_len, act_dim), LOGSTD_MIN, LOGSTD_MAX)
    return mean, torch.exp(log_std)

def distribution(self, obs):
    mean, std = self.forward(obs)
    return D.Independent(D.Normal(mean, std), 2)   # treat (seg_len, act_dim) as one event

def act(self, obs):
    a = self.distribution(np2torch(obs).unsqueeze(0)).sample()
    return torch.clamp(a, -1, 1)[0][0].numpy()     # execute only the first action
Why Independent(Normal, 2) Wrapping a batch of independent Normals and reinterpreting the last 2 dimensions (segment length × action dim) as a single event means log_prob automatically sums the per-coordinate log-densities — giving the joint log-probability of the whole T-step action plan in one call. That's the quantity SFT and DPO need. tanh keeps means in the actuator range; clamping log-std avoids exploding/vanishing variance. All correct.

Plain behavioral cloning / maximum-likelihood on the preferred (winning) action sequences. This produces the reference policy πref that DPO anchors to. Correct.

Q4 · Best-arm identification in a bandit 25 pts

Concept refresher — the setup A multi-armed bandit is a one-state, horizon-1 MDP: pick an arm, see a reward in [0,1], no state transitions. Strategy: pull each of the |A| arms ne times, return the arm with the highest empirical mean r̂a. Question: how many pulls guarantee the returned arm is ε-optimal with probability ≥ 1−δ′? Hoeffding: Pr(|r̂−r| > √(log(2/δ)/(2n))) < δ.

(a) Show Pr(∃a: |r̂a − ra| > √(log(2/δ)/(2ne))) < |A|δ.

Proof (union bound)∎ For a single fixed arm a, Hoeffding with ne samples gives Pr(|r̂a−ra| > √(log(2/δ)/(2ne))) < δ. The event "some arm deviates" is the union of these |A| events, so by the union (Boole) bound
Pr(∃a : |r̂a−ra| > √(log(2/δ)/(2ne))) ≤ Σa Pr(|r̂a−ra| > …) < |A|δ.
(One could tighten by allocating failure probability δ/|A| per arm — the bound below does exactly that.)

(b) How accurately must each arm be estimated, and how many total samples for an ε-optimal arm w.p. ≥ 1−δ′?

Derivation∎ Estimate each arm to within β := ε/2. Define the "good event" G: every arm satisfies |r̂a−ra| ≤ ε/2. Under G, for the returned arm a† = argmax r̂a and the true best a⋆:
ra† ≥ r̂a† − ε2 ≥ r̂a⋆ − ε2 ≥ ( ra⋆ − ε2 ) − ε2 = ra⋆ − ε
(middle step: a† maximizes the empirical mean). So under G the output is ε-optimal. By part (a), Pr(not G) < |A|δ; set |A|δ = δ′, i.e. δ = δ′/|A|. Finally choose ne so the Hoeffding width equals ε/2:
√( log(2/δ)2ne ) = ε2  ⟹  ne = 2ε² log( 2|A|δ′ )
Total pulls across all arms:
N = |A| · ne = 2|A|ε² log( 2|A|δ′ ).
Reading the bound Sample cost scales as 1/ε² (halving the tolerance quadruples the work — the price of statistical confidence), linearly in |A| (more arms to check), and only logarithmically in 1/δ′ (buying more certainty is cheap). This is uniform-allocation pure exploration; adaptive algorithms (e.g. successive elimination) improve the constants by spending fewer pulls on obviously-bad arms.

Q5 · Challenges of RLHF 5 pts

(a) Who are the human raters, and what ethical issues follow?

Generic LLM preference labels are typically produced by low-paid crowdworkers or outsourced contractors, often concentrated in particular countries, English-speaking, and demographically unrepresentative of the global user base. Ethical issues: labor concerns (low pay, precarious work, exposure to disturbing content during safety labeling) and representational bias — the model inherits this narrow group's cultural, political, and stylistic preferences. Impact: outputs that encode a specific worldview as "the" preferred answer, systematically underserving or misrepresenting minority and non-Western perspectives, while appearing neutral.

(b) Would RLHF be a good way to train better medical diagnoses?

Generally no, at least not on lay preferences. RLHF optimizes for what raters prefer, which is not the same as what is correct — raters (even clinicians skimming) tend to favor confident, fluent, reassuring answers, so the model can learn sycophancy and confident wrongness rather than diagnostic accuracy. Medical truth requires verified outcomes and expert-validated labels, not pairwise "which sounds better" comparisons. RLHF could tune bedside tone or formatting, but the diagnostic content should be grounded in validated clinical data with expert oversight.

(c) Describe one more limitation of RLHF.

Reward over-optimization (Goodhart's law). The learned reward model is only an approximation, accurate on the distribution of data it was trained on. As the policy is optimized hard against it, the policy drifts into regions where the reward model is a poor proxy — the measured reward keeps climbing while true quality plateaus or declines (verbose, formulaic, or subtly manipulative outputs that game the reward model). This is why a KL penalty to a reference policy (or DPO's β) is needed: to keep the policy near the region where the proxy is trustworthy. Other valid answers: preference feedback loses information vs. richer signals; single scalar reward can't represent conflicting human values; distribution shift between labeling and deployment.
